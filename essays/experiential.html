EXPERIENTIAL

By George Gondron

EXPERIENTIAL To believe something and to know something These are two different realities We can believe something intellectually But knowing is a matter of experience Relationships, by their very nature, are experiential Since words, in truth, are only labels I can’t know another to any depth through just their words If their words do not match their behavior Trust their behavior, not their words The word love, for example, is an action word We experience love through the actions of others We don’t merely believe they love us, we know But unfortunately, the word love has been assigned to A lower position, now called a feeling Thereby losing its importance as an action Loving a movie, a book, a meal, a trip, an event These are all pleasures, but not love One of the failures of most religions is their inability To stress the importance of an experiential relationship With the One they profess Rather they fall into the trap of rites, rituals, dogma, and doctrine The words of their books become more important than A relationship with the One about whom they teach And love is lost The famous Swiss psychiatrist and psychoanalyst, Carl Jung Was asked toward the end of his life if he believed in God His reply to the journalist was, ‘No, I know’ His knowing surpassed his believing The question in our culture, our time in history becomes From whatever our religious perspective Is our experience grounded in belief or in knowing A daily meditative/contemplative practice is a path to knowing Just a few minutes each day in silence, stillness, and Presence A few minutes to empty our ever-busy ‘monkey minds’ Of their incessant and repetitive thoughts A chance to listen to the whispers of our heart, soul, and spirit To discover our ‘true self’, our inner truth, they wait

George G
